### **Purpose & Context**

“What’s in a name?” - a lot, actually. As Aampe matures and scales, consistent and simplified language isn’t just nice to have; it’s essential to a seamless UX and to how we think, build, and communicate about the system. 

1. Define and **standardize** terminology used across the Aampe platform, documentation, and conversations.
2. Establish a clear **hierarchy** — from atomic units (like alternates) to system-level entities (like audiences or triggers).
3. Encourage shared understanding and **consistent** language across teams (Product, Strategy, Customer, Engineering).

### Achieving Cross-Functional Clarity

1. Propose new terms and test them in-context.
2. Discuss, debate, advocate, and vote!
3. Tie-break, accept, and implement.

### After today, will we have the opportunity to modify terminology?

- YES! Additional terms can be introduced for new functionalities, when needed.
- BUT... modifying agreed-upon terms, after today, will require a rationale and team consensus.
- Prevent terminology creep by using the accepted term over the one you prefer.

### Terminology

| **Term** | **Description** | **Notes** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Alternate** | Smallest dynamic atomic unit of content (text or visual). Example: “Book now!”, a picture, a URL.. | Other options included: Snippet, Variant

Consideration: Might gradually be depreciated as an entity as we move to a ‘whole-cloth’ approach to scaling content generation. |
| **Tag Group** | The category/type of the modular part of a Message, as seen in the current Label Management page. Example: Offering, Value Proposition, Subscription Plans, listed in Label Management | Other options included: Component, Component type, Alternate type, Action set

Consideration: This change will affect Tags, as referred to today.  |
| **Component** | A modular instance of a component type/category that derives from the category/type entities in under Label Management. Example: Greeting, Value Proposition, etc. used in a Message | Other options included: Component instance, Dynamic element

Consideration: Might gradually be depreciated as an entity as we move to a ‘whole-cloth’ approach to scaling content generation. |
| **Tag** | An entity that defines the meaning or intent of textual or visual content. Example: Convenience, Dollar Discount, Tax Filing Off-Season | Other options included: Label, Attribute

Consideration: This change will affect Tags, as referred to today. |
| **Formula** | A collection of related messages variants — the container that encapsulates all the combinations. Example: Abandon Cart - Hotels (EN), DIY Peak Season - Var1 etc | Other options included: Message, Message Group, Recipe |
| **Message** | A single specific combination of variations across components. Example: “Hi there! Start saving on your spends. Check it out” | Other options included: Message Combination |
| **Topic** | The organizing and management layer for content, linking multiple message groups under a shared use case or behavioral goal. Example: Airport Rides, Onboarding | Other options included: Theme |
| **Eligibility Criteria** | Attributes or event-based criteria used to define eligible users. Example: New users signed up in last 14 days, Paid users, Churned users | Other options included: Audience, Eligibility, Eligibility Group |
| **Event** | User actions or system signals that agents learn from. Example: checkout_complete, screen_viewed | Other options included: Behavior |

# Next Steps

With terminology agreed upon conceptually, the next step is to determine **how these terms will be implemented across the Aampe product and ecosystem.** Because these terms are **customer-facing**, introducing them will require coordinated changes across the product interface, documentation, marketing materials, and customer communications.

The **product changes will likely be the largest and most complex part of this effort**, and should therefore be scoped first before broader rollout planning.

---

## 1. Product Impact

Updating terminology in the product will require a detailed assessment of how the new terms map to existing entities, UI elements, backend objects, and workflows.

### Key considerations

- Identify **all areas of the product where current terminology appears**
- Determine how the new terms map to **existing product structures**
- Evaluate whether changes involve **UI-only updates or deeper architectural implications**
- Assess whether **data models, APIs, or backend systems** rely on existing terminology

### Areas likely affected

- Label Management
- Message creation interface
- Content workflows
- Messages Page
- Audience and eligibility configuration
- Reporting or analytics surfaces
- APIs and integrations
- System-generated logs or exports

### Key questions to resolve

- Are these changes **pure renames**, or do they introduce **structural differences** in how the system works?
- Do any existing product concepts **not map cleanly** to the new terminology?
- Are there areas where **legacy terms must remain temporarily** for technical reasons?
- Will these changes require **data migrations or backward compatibility support**?

### Next step

Conduct a **Product + Engineering scoping exercise** to determine:

- scope of UI changes
- engineering complexity
- migration requirements
- dependencies
- rollout sequencing

This assessment should produce a **clear implementation plan and timeline** before external communication begins.

---

## 2. Terminology Mapping & Migration Planning

Once the product impact is understood, the next step is to define how **existing terminology transitions to the new system**.

### Key considerations

- Ensure existing concepts map clearly to new terminology
- Avoid breaking workflows for existing customers
- Ensure continuity for existing content and configurations

### Example mapping exercise

| Current Term | New Term |
| --- | --- |
| Label | Tag |
| Label Type / Component Type | Tag Group |
| Component | Component |
| Message Group | Formula |

This mapping will help guide:

- product updates
- documentation updates
- customer explanations

### Open questions

- Should transitional terminology appear in the UI (e.g., **“Tags (formerly Labels)”**)?
- Should migration occur **all at once** or **incrementally**?

---

## 3. Customer Change Management

Because these terms are customer-facing, existing customers will need support during the transition.

### Key considerations

- Prevent confusion in existing workflows
- Clearly explain terminology changes
- Ensure customers can continue to find familiar concepts

### Possible mechanisms

- product release notes
- updated documentation
- short explainer guides
- customer webinars or walkthroughs

### Key question

- Should terminology changes be released **simultaneously for all customers**, or **rolled out gradually**?

---

## 4. Sales Pipeline Alignment

Prospects currently in the sales pipeline may have already encountered the existing terminology.

### Key considerations

- Ensure consistent messaging across sales conversations
- Avoid introducing confusion mid-evaluation

### Potential approach

- Equip the sales team with a **short terminology explanation slide**
- Clarify the relationship between current and upcoming terms

---

## 5. Website & Marketing Updates

Once terminology is finalized and product implementation is planned, updates will be required across customer-facing materials.

### Areas affected

- website product pages
- product descriptions
- pitch decks
- blog content
- marketing collateral
- customer case studies

### Key consideration

Marketing content should reflect the **same conceptual model used in the product** to maintain consistency.

---

## 6. Knowledge Base & Documentation

Documentation will need to be updated to align with the new terminology.

### Areas affected

- glossary
- product documentation
- training materials
- implementation guides
- onboarding documentation

### Key considerations

- update glossary definitions first
- ensure documentation references the correct product terms
- maintain discoverability during the transition

---

## 7. Rollout Sequencing

Given the scope of change, rollout should follow a clear sequence:

**1. Product & Engineering scoping**

Determine technical feasibility and implementation plan.

**2. Internal alignment**

Ensure all internal teams adopt the new terminology.

**3. Product implementation**

Introduce terminology changes in the product.

**4. Documentation updates**

Update knowledge base and product documentation.

**5. Customer communication**

Explain terminology updates to customers.

**6. Marketing and website updates**

Align external messaging.

---

## 8. Governance Going Forward

To prevent future terminology drift:

- maintain a **centralized terminology reference**
- review new terms introduced with product features
- ensure alignment across product, documentation, and marketing

---

# Risks & Considerations

- **Customer confusion during transition** if familiar terms disappear abruptly.
- **Engineering scope** may be larger than expected depending on where terminology appears in the system.
- **Inconsistent internal adoption** could lead to mixed terminology across teams.
- Some terms (e.g., *Formula*, *Tag Group*) may require explanation for new users.

---

# Validation

Before full rollout, it may be useful to sanity-check terminology with:

- a few customers
- sales and customer strategy teams
- documentation usability reviews.

This helps confirm that the terms are **intuitive and easy to explain in real usage**.
